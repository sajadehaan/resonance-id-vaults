Module 01

Who you are

You're someone who builds belonging out of nothing. You landed in a city where you knew no one, and instead of waiting to be included, you went for a run and asked if anyone wanted to join. "Two people showed up. That was enough." That's not a small thing — most people never post that message, because two people showing up feels like failure. You understood it as a beginning. What you run isn't a training programme. It's a reason to be somewhere at 6am when the city is still quiet. People come back not because they're chasing a race time but because something about "moving through the city while it's still quiet" does something to them that they can't quite explain, and you understand that feeling completely. You document it on film. You soundtrack it carefully. You don't want a sponsor banner. The run is enough — and you mean that in a way most organisers never figure out.

Module 02

Why you do what you do

WHY

Why

Because showing up for strangers — and having them show up for you — is something you didn't know you needed until you had it. You build the thing you wished existed when you arrived somewhere alone.

HOW

How

By refusing to make running a performance. No pace tracking, no sponsors, no bar at the finish. You end wherever the run ends. The aesthetic is deliberate — the routes, the music, the film photography. Everything signals: this is considered, not casual.

WHAT

What

A run club in Tokyo for people who want to feel part of something without it being a whole thing. Twice a week. Sometimes at 6am. Always worth showing up for.

Module 03

Who you attract — and who you don't

Who it's for

People who moved somewhere new and are still figuring it out, or who've been somewhere for years and realise they never actually built a life there. They're curious, a little lost in a good way, and want to feel part of something without it requiring a full identity shift. They care how things feel — the route, the hour, the vibe — not just what they achieve.

Who it's not for

People who need a Strava segment to justify the effort. Those looking for a race crew, a networking event, or a post-run sponsored pint. Anyone who measures a run club's value by its gear partnerships or its Garmin data. If you need a sponsor banner to feel legitimate, this isn't your place.

Module 05

How you sound

Rule 01 Say the specific thing, not the impressive thing.

Do

We meet Tuesdays at 6am at Yoyogi Park south gate. No registration, just show up.

Don't

Join our inclusive community movement experience, open to all fitness levels.

Rule 02 Let the silence do work. Not every sentence needs to close.

Do

Two people showed up the first time. That was enough.

Don't

What started small has grown into an amazing community that proves anyone can start something great!

Rule 03 Write like Four Tet sounds — moving but not rushing.

Do

The city looks different at 6am. You'll see it when you come.

Don't

SIGN UP NOW! Limited spots! Don't miss out on Tokyo's fastest-growing run community!

Module 07

How you summarise yourself

01

The city at 6am is worth it.

Leads with the experience, not the club. Specific to Tokyo's pre-dawn energy — someone who knows will feel it immediately. Doesn't need to explain anything.

02

No pace. No pressure. Just the run.

Directly positions against performance-first run clubs. The triple structure mirrors Four Tet's rhythm — moving but not rushing. Clean and declarative.

03

Built for the ones who just landed.

Speaks directly to the human Jorn described — new to somewhere, figuring it out. Carries warmth without being soft. "Just landed" also works metaphorically for anyone starting over.

04

We end wherever the run ends.

Jorn's own language from the intake. Already a tagline — it captures the anti-structure philosophy in one sentence. Works as a slogan, a caption, a philosophy statement.

05

Show up. That's it. Show up and the rest follows.

Jorn's own words, written exactly as he wrote them. The repetition of "show up" is not a mistake — it's the whole philosophy, doubled down on. The pause after the first two words makes it land.
